Multifunctor means more than it's a functor in each argument.

Let \( C \) and \( D \) be a categories. A bifunctor from \(C\) to \(D\) is defined as a functor from the product category: \( F : C \times C \to D \). Multifunctors are the obvious generalization.

In particular, this implies that for any fixed object \(X\), \(F(X,-)\) and \(F(-,X)\) are functors. But that is not sufficient to define a bifunctor. Let \(f : A \to B \) and \(g : A' \to B' \) be functors in \(C\). The following diagram commutes in \(C \times C\), because morphisms in the product category are defined as paris of morphisms.

$$ \begin{CD} (A,A') @>{1 \times g}>> (A,B')\\ @V{f \times 1}VV @V{f \times 1}VV \\ (B,A') @>{1 \times g}>> (B,B') \end{CD} $$

So the image must commute in \(D\)

$$ \begin{CD} F(A,A') @>{F(1 \times g)}>> F(A,B')\\ @V{F(f \times 1)}VV @V{F(f \times 1)}VV \\ F(B,A') @>{F(1 \times g)}>> F(B,B') \end{CD} $$
$$ \newcommand{\catname}[1]{\text{#1}} $$ But it's not sufficient that \(F\) be a functor in each argument to guarantee that this will be the case.

Here is a counterexample. Let \(C\) be any nonabelian group \(G\), so \(C\) has one object and the morphisms are the group elements.
Let \(D = \catname{Set}\)
Let \( F(\cdot,\cdot) = G \)
For \(f,g \in G\) let \(F(f,g) : G \to G\) take \(x \mapsto fgx \)

For the image diagram above to commute, that would mean that for any \(f,g \in G\) we would have \( fg = gf \)
